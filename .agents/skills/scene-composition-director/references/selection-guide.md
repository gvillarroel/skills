# Scene Composition Selection Guide

Use this guide to choose composition approaches per scene. The goal is not to pick a pretty layout; it is to match the scene's job, source material, viewer task, timing, and constraints to a frame structure that can be validated.

## Selection Order

Choose in this order:

1. **Scene job**: hook, problem, product intro, mechanism, feature, comparison, proof, data claim, transition, breather, CTA, or outro.
2. **Viewer task**: read one idea, compare two states, trace a path, understand a mechanism, inspect real media, feel scale, trust proof, or act.
3. **Source strength**: real product footage/screenshot, supplied image, code/data, narration-only idea, text quote, or no asset.
4. **Information density**: one hero idea, 2-3 grouped ideas, 4-8 items, dense labels/data, or multi-stage process.
5. **Frame constraints**: landscape, portrait, square, captions, brand palette, legibility, and any keep-out regions.
6. **Sequence context**: previous and next scene. Vary armature unless repetition is the point.
7. **Motion need**: reveal to narration, demonstrate transformation, land a held read, or create a transition-ready handoff.

## Composition Families

### Centered Hero

Use for hooks, product names, coined terms, single claims, climax frames, clean CTAs, and short held reads.

Choose when the viewer must remember one thing. Reject when the scene has multiple peers, dense labels, or a real interface that needs inspection. Keep the hero large enough to pass the squint test and add supporting structure so it does not become a sparse slide.

### Rule Of Thirds

Use for one hero plus supporting context, narration-led explainers, presenter-like framing, or scenes that need elegant negative space.

Choose when the support element is important but subordinate. Reject when equality between sides matters. Put the focal point on a thirds intersection and place text in the opposing clean field.

### Split Screen

Use for before/after, A/B, two options, two forces, source/output, or two complementary capabilities.

Choose when both sides need comparable weight. Reject for three or more items, one dominant hero, or process flow. Keep mirrored hierarchy unless one side is intentionally the winner.

### Asymmetric Editorial

Use for dense diagrams, asset plus caption rail, product surface plus explanation, or scenes that need tension and sophistication.

Choose when one side carries the hero and the other side explains, scores, or labels it. Reject when the scene should feel calm and balanced. Good ratios: 60/40 or 70/30, with strong alignment.

### Grid Or Modular Board

Use for feature sets, logo walls, checklists, dashboards, matrices, sortable modules, repeated cards, or proof breadth.

Choose when the viewer needs to scan peers. Reject when the scene has a single emotional focal point. Use stable tracks and gutters so motion cannot resize the composition.

### Full-Width Strip

Use for timelines, pipelines, number lines, route maps, subtitles as structure, or horizontal process beats.

Choose when order matters more than hierarchy. Reject for portrait unless converted into a vertical strip. Keep labels readable and reserve enough height for the band to feel intentional.

### Layered Depth

Use for immersive openings, abstract metaphors, cinematic product moments, 3D/WebGL scenes, particle fields, and atmosphere around one idea.

Choose when mood and spatial travel matter. Reject when the viewer must read many small labels. Specify at least background, midground, and foreground roles; otherwise the depth is only decoration.

### Diagonal Armature

Use for change, escalation, conflict, progress, transformation, critical paths, and before-to-after stories.

Choose when the scene has direction. Put source or old state low-left and outcome or target high-right. Reject for calm comparison or centered brand lockups.

### Radial Or Hub

Use for cycles, ecosystems, hub-and-spoke products, peer categories around one center, orbiting proof, or weighted wheels.

Choose when the center has a real semantic role. Reject when there is no true center. Reserve outer lanes for labels so the ring does not become decorative clutter.

### Flow Spine

Use for pipelines, model execution, source-to-output stories, handoffs, sequence lifelines, decision paths, and process explainers.

Choose when the viewer must trace cause and effect. Reject for unrelated peers. Keep branches secondary and return them to the spine.

### Dense Label Lanes

Use for maps, point fields, document annotations, crowded diagrams, dense scatter, and any scene where labels must remain inspectable.

Choose when labels would otherwise collide with the data field. Move labels to side/top/bottom/ring lanes and connect with short leaders. Reject for simple hero scenes.

### Golden Or Root Split

Use when one visual field is dominant and a smaller field explains, summarizes, or constrains it: product surface plus notes, chart plus legend, document plus score, code plus callout.

Choose when the supporting field should never compete with the hero. Reject when the two fields are equal peers.

When the smaller field is a magnified detail or proof panel, connect it to the source region with a visible lens, leader, or shared motion path. Otherwise the support field can read as an unrelated UI card even when its label is correct. Animate the source region and the magnified field in the same phase when the viewer must understand causality.

## Source-Based Choices

- **Real product UI or footage**: prefer product-surface showcase, asymmetric editorial, golden/root split, or picture-in-picture. Keep the real asset inspectable. Do not cover faces, key UI, controls, or brand marks with text.
- **No real asset, narration-only concept**: invent the hero. Use type-led, abstract metaphor, diagram, data-viz, or flow spine. Do not use generic decorative blobs as the concept.
- **Data or metric claim**: use centered stat, data panel, flow spine, diagonal escalation, or golden/root chart plus explanation. Preserve quantitative relationships.
- **Code or PR change**: use flow spine for cause/effect, split screen for before/after, grid for changed files, dense label lanes for diffs, or centered hero for the release headline.
- **Music or beat-led visual**: choose by rhythm first. Use centered hero for hits, grid/strip for repeated beats, radial for loops, and layered depth for sustained sections.

## Aspect Ratio Choices

- **Landscape**: supports split screen, wide strip, asymmetric 60/40, grids, and panoramic flow.
- **Portrait**: stack vertically. Convert split screen to top/bottom, full-width strip to vertical spine, and grid to fewer columns. Keep the hero around the upper-middle, leaving bottom caption space.
- **Square**: supports centered hero, two-column split for compact peers, radial, grid, and layered depth. Avoid wide timelines unless simplified.

## Motion And Rhythm Choices

- Use **VO-paced reveal** when narration names pieces in sequence. Only show what the voiceover has reached.
- Use **held read** for climax, proof, CTA, or a deliberately quiet breather. Held scenes are valid when they are allocated in video direction.
- Use **development motion** for mechanisms, data builds, process flow, and transformation. The mid-shot change should teach the point.
- Use **scene-specific motion** when multiple scenes share a style. A repeated background sweep, pulse, orbit, or ambient layer must keep the same semantic role in every scene; otherwise replace it with motion produced by that scene's own armature, such as a route in a network, split in a branch, fill in a proof chart, or completion in a radial checklist.
- Avoid **slideshow failure**: entrance finishes early and the frame freezes by the first quarter.
- Avoid **screensaver failure**: unrelated ambient motion continues without serving attention or meaning.

## Multi-Scene Armature Sets

When a sequence is meant to test or show composition range, name a distinct armature per scene and preserve continuity through lower-level vocabulary such as palette, token identity, type, spacing, or motion verbs. Do not make a validation-only motion layer the cross-scene connector. If automated video checks need more pixel change, add semantic movement inside each scene's diagram rather than a shared decorative background.

## Validation Questions

Before finalizing each scene, answer:

- What is the single focal element after a squint test?
- What is second in hierarchy, and why does it not compete?
- Which armature organizes the eye?
- Where is text allowed, and what area is reserved for captions or key assets?
- What are the background, midground, and foreground layers?
- Which visual pieces appear in each phase, and what changes mid-shot?
- Which nearby composition did you reject, and why?
- Does any repeated motion carry the same role in every scene, or is it only keeping the frame active?
